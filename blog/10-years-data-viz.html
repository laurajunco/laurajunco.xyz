    10 years of Data Visualization

    It has been around 10 years since I first learned about Data Visualization. Since then, I've honed the skills of representing the data of our world through pixels on a screen, while working on a great variety of disciplines and geographical places.

    When I first started, I would have liked to read the path that took different people to finding their way to Dataviz, so I thought I would share some of my experiences today, and hope that it is useful or inspiring for someone out there

    Design for Interactive Media

    In my 2nd year of university I took the first course that included any type of coding. I remember it vividly. In Design for Interactive Media, we learnt the principles of computer programming from the lens of art and design. We used Processing (still one of my favorite programming tools), and every week we were tasked with one creative assignment where we explored one computational concept (and that's how I learnt about for loops, but also how digital color works, or about Ani Albers).

    I felt like I had discovered a secret: how to put the power of computation at the service of creative ideas. I was very lucky to have Hernando Barragán as my teacher, and over the years as I came back as a teaching assistant for this class, I learnt more and more from him.

    The final course assignment was (you guessed right) a Data Visualization assignment! I wanted to explore Data Physicalization, and represent the amount of drinking water available for the different countries of the world. The concept was simple, one bottle per country, how much water is in it represents the amount of drinkable water available per capita. I still remember both my parents had to help me fill, label and carry the bottles to Uni when I realised I had been too ambitious with my goal.

    Data Visualization for Scientific Research

    I spent the next years of college education diving deeper into coding, and I was loving it. Somehow came a super rare opportunity for a Data Visualization intern position at the EBI. So I made my way to the UK, and joined the Chembl group, which publishes a database used by researchers all around the world to find information about drug-like compounds! Which is very important if you are trying to make medicines.

    The learning curve was steep but exponential, from hearing about git for the first time to redesigning the full web-interface. I arrived with basic html and css knowledge and left having a full grasp of what contributing to a big software project looks like. Alongside my mentor here, Daviz Mendez, we crafted so many complex web visualizations to enable the visual exploration of this huge database available to the scientific community. What I enjoyed the most was the exchange with the scientists here, and really seeing how technology also plays a huge role in science and science communication

    Data Visualization for Peace

    Coming back to Colombia, very exciting things were happening. After more than 60 years of political violence, a big peace treaty was being signed. It was time for hope and excitement about peace and building a better future for the country.

    I was lucky to join the Truth Commission of Colombia. If you are unfamiliar with the term, a Truth Commission is an organization created with the sole purpose of researching and revealing what happened in countries during eras of conflict, to provide recommendations for a lasting peace.

    I worked alongside expert analysts, statisticians and social scientists, to make sense of hundreds of thousands of interviews, legal documents and text-based data. I created custom visualizations for both internal analysis and public research. The data itself was very tough to work with as most of it revolved around war crimes and its consequences, but every single person was completely engaged in doing the best work possible for the future of Colombia.

    Even though most of my work did not make it to the public eye, it is single handedly the most meaningful project I've ever been a part of. Once again I was very happy that Data Visualization was useful to make sense of real events and helped researchers understand, and communicate their findings. I got the chance to learn and work alongside Néstor and Juan, who apart from their incredible technical skills have worked for years on peace-making and cultural projects.

    Data Visualization Software for others

    Afterwards, I was hungry for diving deeper into more technical topics, and was very interested in Data Visualization tools and how they are made. That's how 4 years ago I joined the Celonis Data Visualization team, where we build Dataviz tools for other engineers and maintain a low-code Dashboard building software for Data Analysts. I was super lucky to join Philipp's team, a true on-hands leader with both technical and domain knowledge, guiding our operations.

    For the first time, my official title was ‘Software Developer’ and I jumped head first into a big tech company engineering organization. I truly had to get more technical, but have worked on the most exciting projects I’ve ever contributed to. We built a charting library for other engineers to create out-of-the-box visualizations, work on complex graphs to visualize how a company's processes work, and build the editing experience so that analysts can build custom dashboards for their companies.

    Working on a big software project with hundreds of engineers contributing daily, has really taught me about the craft of tool-building and maintenance. Building things that last and that are improved and fixed over time, and building tools that enable others to create and make sense of thor own data

    So what

    Nowadays there are a lot of things happening, but I still believe making sense of data can help us understand our environment and become better as a society. These 10 years have been a wild but rewarding ride, and I'm very grateful for all the people that have guided me and have let me learn from them. I hope to keep contributing and learning to this field, and I hope to see how things are 10 years from now!
