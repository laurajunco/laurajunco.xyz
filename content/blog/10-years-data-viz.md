# 10 years of Data Visualization

It’s been around 10 years since I first learned about Data Visualization. Since then, I've honed the skills of representing the world’s data through pixels on a screen while working on a great variety of disciplines.

When I first started, I would have liked to read the path that led people to find their way to Dataviz, so I'm sharing some of my experiences today, and hope that it is useful or inspiring for someone out there.

## Design for Interactive Media 

In my 2nd year of university, I took the first course that included any type of coding. I remember it vividly. In *Design for Interactive Media*, we learnt the principles of computer programming through the lens of art and design. We used [Processing](https://processing.org/) (still one of my favorite programming tools), and every week we had one creative assignment to explore one computational concept (and that's how I learnt about for loops, but also how digital color works, or about [Ani Albers](https://www.albersfoundation.org/tags/textiles)). 

I felt I had discovered a secret: how to put the power of computation at the service of creative ideas. I was very lucky to have [Hernando](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hernando_Barrag%C3%A1n) as my teacher, and over the years, as I came back as a teaching assistant for this course, I learnt more and more from him.

The final course assignment was (you guessed right\!) a Data Visualization assignment. I wanted to explore Data Physicalization and represent the availability of drinking water around the world. The concept was simple: one bottle per country, how much water is in it represented the amount of drinking water available per capita. I still remember both my parents having to help me fill, label, and carry hundreds of bottles to Uni when I realised I had been too ambitious with my project.

## Data Visualization for Scientific Research

I spent the next years diving deeper into coding, and I was loving it. Somehow, came a super rare opportunity for a Data Visualization internship at [EBI](https://www.ebi.ac.uk/). So I made my way to the UK and joined the [Chembl](https://www.ebi.ac.uk/chembl/) group, which publishes a database used all around the world to find information about drug-like compounds\! Which is very important if you are a scientist trying to make new medicines, for example.

The learning curve was steep but exponential, from hearing about *git* for the first time to redesigning the full web interface. I arrived with basic HTML and CSS knowledge and left having a full grasp of what contributing to a big software project looks like. Alongside my mentor here, David, we crafted so many complex web visualizations to enable the visual exploration of this huge database available to the scientific community. What I enjoyed the most was the exchange with the scientists and seeing how technology plays a huge role in scientific communication.

## Data Visualization for Peace

Coming back to Colombia, very exciting things were happening. After more than 60 years of political violence, a big peace treaty was being signed. It was time for hope and excitement about peace and building a better future for the country. 

I was lucky to join the [Truth Commission of Colombia](https://www.comisiondelaverdad.co/). If you are unfamiliar with the term, a [Truth Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_commission) is an organization that researches and reveals what happened in [countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_truth_and_reconciliation_commissions) during eras of conflict, to provide recommendations for a lasting peace.

I worked alongside expert analysts, statisticians, and social scientists to interpret hundreds of thousands of interviews, legal documents, and text-based data. I created custom visualizations for both internal analysis and public research. The data itself was very tough, as most of it revolved around war crimes and their consequences, but every single person was deeply engaged in doing the best work possible for the future of our country. 

Even though most of my work did not make it to the public eye, it is single-handedly the most meaningful project I've ever been a part of. 

Once again, I saw Data Visualization as a means to make sense of real events and help researchers communicate their findings. I got the chance to learn alongside [Néstor](https://github.com/nestorandrespe) and [Juan](https://arroyocreativa.com/), who, apart from their incredible technical skills, worked for years on peace-making and cultural projects.

## Data Visualization Software for others

Afterwards, I was hungry for diving deeper into more technical topics, and was very interested in Data Visualization tools and how they are made. That's how 4 years ago I joined the [Celonis](https://www.celonis.com/) Data Visualization team, where we make Dataviz tools for engineers and maintain a low-code dashboard building software for Data Analysts. I joined [Philipp](https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=pURMhdUAAAAJ&hl=de)'s team, a true on-hands leader with both technical and domain knowledge, guiding our operations.

For the first time, my official title was *Software Developer*, and I jumped headfirst into a big tech company engineering organization. I truly had to get more technical, and worked on the most exciting projects I’ve ever contributed to. We built a charting library for engineers to create out-of-the-box visualizations, craft complex graphs to visualize companies processes, and maintain the editing experience so that analysts can create custom dashboards for their companies. 

Contributing to a software project with hundreds of engineers contributing daily has really taught me about the craft of tool-making and [maintenance](https://arena-attachments.s3.amazonaws.com/14282920/cac82de5a6c58b067b49d14d0b036ac8.pdf?1638930502). Building things that last and are improved and fixed over time, and building tools that enable others to create and understand their own data.

## So what

Nowadays, there are a lot of things happening, but I still believe making sense of data can help us understand our environment and become better as a society. These 10 years have been a wild but rewarding ride, and I'm very grateful for all the people who have guided me and let me learn from them. I hope to keep contributing and learning in this field, and I’m curious to see how things will be 10 years from now.
